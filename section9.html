469 days. Seven months out, seven months back, and 63 days on the surface of another world.

From here, Earth fills half the porthole. Blue and white, impossibly vivid. The crew hasn't spoken in ten minutes. They're just looking.

The capsule begins to orientate. Blunt end forward. They'll hit atmosphere at 10.2 kilometres per second — 36,700 km/h — and the air in front of them will compress so violently it glows.

Mission Control goes quiet. For the next 7 minutes 42 seconds, plasma will surround the capsule and block every signal. No comms. No telemetry. Just silence on Earth, and fire in the sky.

In 47 cities, spontaneous crowds gathered in public squares to watch the live feed. In schools from Lagos to Oslo, children had tracked the mission on classroom walls for 14 months.

Six people had walked on Mars and come back.

The crew spent their first night aboard the recovery vessel. Dr. Okafor called her mother. Kowalski ate three plates of food and laughed until she fell asleep at the table.

Mission Commander Chen gave a brief statement from the ship:

"We went. We looked. We learned. And we came back to tell you — it is worth going further."
